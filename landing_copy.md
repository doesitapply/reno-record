## SECTION 2 — THE BACKSTORY (cover story)

**Title:** How a man who asked to represent himself ended up more prepared than the people trying to convict him.

In March 2023, Cameron Church was arrested in Washoe County, Nevada. The charges were serious. The case should have moved quickly. It did not.

What followed was over three years of procedural delay, contested competency proceedings, counsel substitutions without clear authority, filings that were stricken from the record without rulings, and a speedy trial clock that kept running while the court kept resetting it.

Church invoked his right to self-representation under *Faretta v. California*, 422 U.S. 806 (1975) — a constitutional right recognized by the U.S. Supreme Court. The court's handling of that invocation is documented in this archive.

He filed public records requests. Most went unanswered or were denied without legal basis. Those are documented too.

He built this system — the archive, the AI pipeline, the violation tracker — while in custody. Not to win sympathy. To create a record that cannot be disputed, altered, or ignored.

The irony: if they had simply let him represent himself when he first asked, this archive would not exist.

## SECTION 4 — WHAT THE RECORD SHOWS (violation categories, cited)

Each of the following categories represents a documented pattern in the reviewed record. Each is tied to specific legal authority.

### 1. Speedy Trial / Delay
**Authority:** NRS 178.556 (Nevada 60-day rule for in-custody defendants); U.S. Const. amend. VI; *Barker v. Wingo*, 407 U.S. 514 (1972).
**What the record shows:** The reviewed record documents repeated continuances, reset speedy trial clocks, and periods of delay that the record does not fully account for with defendant-attributable cause.

### 2. Faretta / Self-Representation Rights
**Authority:** *Faretta v. California*, 422 U.S. 806 (1975); *Indiana v. Edwards*, 554 U.S. 164 (2008); NRS 178.388.
**What the record shows:** The reviewed record documents invocations of the right to self-representation and subsequent proceedings that do not clearly reflect a *Faretta* colloquy or a ruling on the invocation.

### 3. Competency Proceeding Abuse
**Authority:** *Pate v. Robinson*, 383 U.S. 375 (1966); NRS 178.400–178.460; *Drope v. Missouri*, 420 U.S. 162 (1975).
**What the record shows:** The reviewed record documents competency proceedings initiated at procedurally significant moments. The record does not clearly locate all predicate findings required to initiate or extend such proceedings.

### 4. Brady / Discovery Issue
**Authority:** *Brady v. Maryland*, 373 U.S. 83 (1963); *Giglio v. United States*, 405 U.S. 150 (1972); NRS 174.235.
**What the record shows:** The reviewed record documents requests for discovery materials that the record does not confirm were produced.

### 5. Due Process Defect
**Authority:** U.S. Const. amend. XIV; *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976); NRS 1.030.
**What the record shows:** The reviewed record documents procedural events — stricken filings, ignored motions, orders without apparent hearings — that the record does not fully support with locatable predicate authority.

### 6. Judicial Disqualification / Bias
**Authority:** NRS 1.230; Canon 2, Nevada Code of Judicial Conduct; *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.*, 556 U.S. 868 (2009).
**What the record shows:** The reviewed record documents recusal motions and judicial conduct that the record does not fully address with required findings.

### 7. Prosecutorial Misconduct
**Authority:** *Berger v. United States*, 295 U.S. 78 (1935); Nevada RPC 3.8; *United States v. Young*, 470 U.S. 1 (1985).
**What the record shows:** The reviewed record documents prosecutorial conduct that the record does not fully reconcile with the obligations of a minister of justice.

### 8. Public Records Noncompliance
**Authority:** NRS 239.010 et seq. (Nevada Public Records Act); *Reno Newspapers, Inc. v. Gibbons*, 127 Nev. 625 (2011).
**What the record shows:** 14 public records requests are documented in this archive. The reviewed record does not locate timely, complete, or legally sufficient responses to the majority of them.

## SECTION 5 — THE EMBARRASSING PART (court-safe, sourced)

**Title:** What happens when you document everything.

The record contains moments that are difficult to explain.

A competency evaluation was ordered at a moment when the defendant had just filed a detailed, legally-cited motion. The record does not locate a predicate finding of doubt sufficient to trigger NRS 178.415 at that specific juncture.

A Faretta invocation appears in the record. The record does not locate a subsequent colloquy, a ruling granting or denying the request, or a finding of waiver. The case continued with appointed counsel.

Filings were stricken. The record does not locate rulings explaining the legal basis for striking them.

A speedy trial clock ran. Continuances were granted. The record does not always locate clear defendant-attributable cause for the delays, as required by *Barker v. Wingo*.

None of this is alleged. It is documented. The documents are in this archive. You can read them.

## SECTION 6 — WHY IT MATTERS

This is one case. But the patterns documented here — competency proceedings used as delay mechanisms, Faretta rights ignored, discovery withheld, public records stonewalled — are not unique to Washoe County.
